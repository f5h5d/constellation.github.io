Reflection

Before going on this field trip, I knew the importance of the environment and how we have to save it, but I honestly did not care that much about it. Before this field trip, I had never gone camping, if you can call this camping, or ever been sitting around a fireplace telling stories. It was a very fun experience and one that was enhanced by the beauty of nature. When I first heard about the English trip, it was not my intention to go, but I decided otherwise due to a friend’s compelling argument as to why it was a trip worth experiencing. She enthusiastically expressed how the trip was fun when with friends and how the activities were interactive and enjoyable. Her reasons convinced me and I am thankful for her convincing because I also found the trip to be very enjoyable. My favourite part was definitely when we were all near the fire and were being told star stories. Although I do not remember exactly what was said in the stories, I still do remember the experience itself. Being in complete darkness with only the light and sound of the fire was calming and soothing; in other words, it was therapeutic. The landscape and scenery were amazing and I began to realise why we should try to protect the environment at all costs: it houses trillions of species, helps us as humans, and is just beautiful to look at. This was my first time being out in the wilderness with a campfire, and I feel like it helped me connect with Indigenous people more because I understood at that moment their belief in the interconnectedness of people and nature. They understand that human actions directly affect nature, and they make sure to waste as little as possible. Additionally, through sharing star stories, we are upholding the personal sovereignty of the Indigenous peoples. We are recognizing and endorsing the different world views that Indigenous peoples have, and the traditions they follow by learning more about them. By putting Indigenous teachings/learnings into the curriculum, we are spreading their culture and getting rid of racist stereotypes that are put on them; all of the stereotypes mentioned in the poem “Future We In-U-Wee” are examples of racism. Thinking about star stories reminds me of an Inuit story we looked at in class: the story of Sedna. Sedna was the daughter of two Giants, but she quickly outgrew them to the point where there was not enough food on the land to satisfy her hunger. Because of this, her parents took her to the middle of the ocean and threw her overboard, cutting off each of her fingers when she grabbed the edge of the canoe when she tried to save herself. Each cut-off finger turned into a different sea creature, and she became the goddess of the sea. This story relates not only to Inuit peoples but Indigenous peoples in general as it portrays the importance the Indigenous peoples put on their relationship with nature. All the activities we did were done outside, and the reason for that is because of how important nature is in Indigenous cultures. Through our experiences outside, I realised the importance of nature and why the Indigenous peoples view it as sacred. The story of Sedna shows the give-and-take relationship the Indigenous peoples have with it, when the Indigenous peoples take care of the land, the land/sea gives back to them in the form of resources and food. After experiencing the field trip, I have gained more appreciation for the beauty of nature and how important it is. The field trip captures a portion of Indigenous culture and it is something I believe everyone should experience as it is an eye-opening experience.

Reflection

I originally thought of Indigenous and Islamic beliefs to be entirely different, but through Two-Eyed Seeing, I recognized similarities and even learned something new about my religion. I identify as a Muslim, and although my faith differs from most Indigenous ideologies, I respect them for their devotion. In the Two-Eyed-Seeing video, Rebecca Thomas talks about how it “takes the strengths of both a colonised world and both an Indigenous world” (Thomas 8:38 - 8:44). This reminded me of a belief in my religion called Shura: before making a major decision, the chief meets with a counsel of people who share their different perspectives on the matter to discuss the pros and cons of the decision. I see a relationship between Two-Eyed Seeing and Shura in that they both take multiple perspectives into account. They are both concepts that are great for decision-making and would have been great tools when it decides on creating residential schools. Although unrealistic, if the Canadian government had taken the principle of multiple perspectives from Two-Eyed Seeing and Shura, they would have been met with negative opinions. Additionally, if they had used the same principle when creating “The Boy In The Picture” photo, they would have quickly realised its faults and how terrible it is. Although the government realised their mistake later, Thomas Moore Keesick and many other Indigenous children had already suffered and it could have been prevented if they had tried to understand the Indigenous cultures and realise that they are not barbaric, but peaceful. Additionally, while researching Two-Eyed Seeing, I found an image of an oddly familiar-eyed-shaped amulet online. I originally thought it would be an Indigenous artefact, but through more research, I found that it was an Islamic symbol: one that is used for protection against the ‘evil eye’, a symbol of malevolent glares. Although completely different, I found it interesting what the concept of seeing can be symbolic of in both cultures: in one it represents the importance of seeing things from different perspectives, while in the other it represents a curse related to jealousy. Through further research, I found a plethora of even more similarities between the two faiths, and through these similarities, I found a new way to connect with Indigenous peoples. For me, Two-Eyed Seeing bridged the gap between Indigenous and Islamic beliefs, revealing unexpected connections.

Reflection

Personal sovereignty was not a concept I was aware of before this unit, but as I learned more, I realised how important having personal sovereignty is to me: I like having control over my life and decision-making. I took it for granted and did not appreciate how not having control over my decisions could impact my identity: I could not imagine living a life where I was denied education or the freedom of choosing my career path. Unfortunately, the poem “Future We In-U-Wee” by Dr. Norma Dunning outlines the lack of freedom that Inuit people have over their own life: they are told to never “dream of a future“ or “make a better life for [themselves]” (Dunning 50-51). Dunning speaks about the lack of support or personal sovereignty that Inuit people are given. Despite the lack of infrastructure or job opportunities that Inuit communities receive, the Canadian government has been batting an eye similar to how they have been sweeping Thomas Moore Keesick’s story under the rug. Thinking in the shoes of an Inuit person, even if I wanted to take control of my own life by trying to get a higher education, I would not be given ample opportunities to obtain a quality education, directly reducing the likelihood of getting employed, and further reducing my freedom. The current Canadian government is catering to the majority of people and paying no attention to the Inuit people, but also Indigenous peoples as a whole. If they were to utilise the Indigenous concept of Two-Eyed-Seeing, where they take the perspective of both themselves and also the Indigenous peoples, they would understand how unfair it must feel to live as “Canada’s biggest afterthought” (50). From the perspective of an Inuit person, they have done nothing wrong to deserve this terrible treatment. They have had a history filled with mental and physical trauma alongside having families ripped apart and despite how many years have passed, they are still treated as a burden. It is not a coincidence that the suicide rate of Inuit people is up to 25 times more than the general population. With the lack of any therapy or support at all, I truthfully could see myself breaking and giving in if I were to live in such an oppressive system. Through the constant repetition that Dunning uses, I feel more impact from each of these lines. The repetition of “freak” creates a mood of not belonging and being treated as an outsider, which is ironic in that the Inuit are one of if not the first known inhabitants of Canada: they are being treated as outsiders in their own country. Additionally, the poem exhibits reflective tones of frustration and resentment when discussing the harsh treatment and racism that Inuit people face constantly. We as the next generation need to change the way Inuit people are viewed and give back their sovereignty: we need to treat them as people and give them access to higher education. Through Dunning’s poem, I have learned about the lack of say that Inuit people have had in their life and how lucky I am to be able to choose to go to a university or to be able to afford a house. These things that seemed normal to me are a challenge for many Inuit people who are treated unjustly. Moving forward I will not take anything I have for granted.

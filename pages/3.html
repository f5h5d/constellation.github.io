Reflection

Before watching the podcast episode, “The Boy In The Picture '', I would never have guessed that it would bring political feelings out of me and help me to begin to understand my political identity. When hearing the title, for some odd reason it felt like a title that I had heard before: I had to search it up out of curiosity and when I did, I saw an iconic image, one that I had seen many times in the past. Despite having seen it so many times, I struggled to remember anything about the person, or what the image was for. But because of my ignorance, I was jumping out of my skin while listening to the podcast. I learned that the image was “two black and white pictures taken years apart featuring the same child with a title that reads before and after… His name was Thomas Moore Keesick” (The Secret Life Of Canada 0:20 - 0:29). The tone of the authors used when discussing the whole situation was overall negative. This negative tone influenced me to care more about this issue as without their negative tone, I would have forgotten about the podcast episode. The sadness and disgust that they displayed is something I still remember. Hearing about his name, his tragic story, and how the government is trying to cover it up was all very saddening. Learning how the government has been sweeping this whole situation under a rug was honestly both horrific and irritating: I wondered in my head if the current government is as inclusive and for the people as they claim to be, “surely there is someone that could better lead and represent this country?” I thought to myself. The great exposition of the authors helped tremendously when trying to understand their viewpoints. The background information that they provided was very helpful to me as it gave me context on the situation at hand that helped me to formulate an opinion: Thomas’s Indigenous identity was taken away from him, and transformed into one of a white person: he was whitewashed. His name, his culture, and his heritage were all stripped away and forgotten: the only evidence of his existence is the propaganda image. Thomas was dehumanised, similar to the dehumanisation in Hunting By Stars when the captured Indigenous people are referred to as numbers instead of actual names. They are stripped of human contact, treated worse than animals, and talked about like merchandise. The government in Hunting By Stars heavily alludes to the government that was present in the time of Thomas and thus he was treated just as terribly as the residents. But, the fact that Thomas’s name is being spread more in recent times brings hope for the future and the potential for the government to have no choice but to acknowledge Thomas’s past. Through this podcast episode, I learned about a more politically critical side of my identity which led to me doing more research about the different political parties and what they stand for to get a better grasp and understanding of where I stand. I appreciate the creators of the podcast trying to bring this issue into the limelight and exposing yet another terrible thing that we as Canada should be ashamed of.

Reflection

Hunting By Stars was written in a very different direction from where I thought a sequel to The Marrow Thieves would go: instead of being a story of payback that I thought it would be, it challenged morality and further dives into the topic of identity. My reasoning for my thinking is that during the end of The Marrow Thieves when the group was trying to save Minerva, I was getting feelings of reclamation, so I assumed the sequel would build on that, but I was wrong. When Frenchie is put into the terrible predicament of having to choose between sacrificing two of ‘his people’ to return to his family, or remaining at the facility and losing his Metis identity, I felt terror: thinking about what I would do in Frenchie’s shoes made my brain hurt. Frenchie’s decision of sacrifice gave me mixed feelings: happiness that he might see his family, but sadness that he would sacrifice two of ‘his own’. There is irony in that Frenchie’s overarching goal is to preserve his culture, but he chooses to sacrifice two Indigenous people for his objective. This ironic situation helped me to learn more about Frenchie’s personality as you learn the most about a person's morals when they are in a tough situation. Therese’s reaction of “What the f*** is going on” (Dimaline 246) created a tone of awkwardness that made me cringe so hard I had to put the book down. This tone adds the majority of the intensity that I felt while reading this whole section of the novel. Additionally, after just Sunny remained near Frenchie, “she was gazing up at [him] again—no judgement, no fear, just wide-open eyes” (247). I was having more mixed feelings due to this dilemma: I felt guilty that Sunny was going to be sacrificed, but I was happy that Frenchie was going to escape. I rarely feel this type of emotion, but whenever I do, negative feelings are felt no matter which decision is made: it is just a terrible situation. After finishing the chapter, I took a moment to reflect on what I had just read: I thought about what I would do in Frenchie’s situation. I concluded that although it would be difficult, I would choose to do the same as Frenchie did: sacrifice people I do not know for my benefit. Through my reflection, I learned that I might be more of an opportunist than I had originally thought. Going back to the novel, this whole section is a massive morality question similar to the trolly dilemma, but Frenchie’s situation is a little different as his decision can be interpreted as the emerging topic of greed. Frenchie greedily chose himself over the lives of two people, choosing individual freedom over collective survival. This is a decision that will stay with Frenchie forever and will have a major effect on his identity as a Metis person.
